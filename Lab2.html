Problem 1¶

In lecture and homework we explored how the convolution can be used to calculate the probability of a sum or average. For this problem we are going to imagine that we are looking for gamma-ray sources (e.g. with the Fermi telescope). In this kind of telescope there is a background of cosmic-rays (electrons and protons, mostly) that provides a discrete noise term across the sky that precisely follows a Poisson distribution. To detect a gamma-ray source, you need to ask what is the probability that the cosmic-ray background would have given you a measurement as signal-like or more than the signal that you received.

To set up the problem, assume in 1 day the average cosmic-ray background is some number X (pick someting btwn 0.5 and 10, with different values for you and your lab partner); and the average number of gamma-rays emitted by your hypothetical source is Y (pick something larger than X).

The distribution for day 5, plotted above, is still a Poisson distribution. Mathematically, since the values that were added together are integers (discrete) the sum of integers will still be integers, so this distribution is still discrete. Conceptually, our background data is cosmic rays, which are discrete - it makes no sense to talk about half a cosmic ray, so the sum of cosmic ray data must also be discrete.

As shown in part B, and by the log plot for the average over 5 days, the average probability distribution still looks discrete (Poisson) and highly skewed on the log plot. However, as we average over more days the log plot looks more like an upside-down parabola, or looks more Guassian. By 2000 days, the log plot of the probability distribution for the average of cosmic rays (that follow a Poisson distribution if we're only looking at one day) looks quite Gaussian, which is consistent with the Central Limit Theorem which states that the sum or average of many independent samples of a random variable, which may not be normal, is approximately a normal random variable.
